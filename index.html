  • Is my bleeding normal?
    Light to moderate bleeding (lochia) is normal for up to 6 weeks postpartum. Watch out for large clots, foul odor, or soaking a pad in under an hour. (ACOG, 2020)
  • Why do I still look pregnant?
    Your uterus takes about 6‚Äì8 weeks to return to its pre-pregnancy size. Mild abdominal bloating and retained fluid are also normal. (Mayo Clinic, 2022)
  • I feel sad but I‚Äôm not sure why. Is this normal?
    Yes. Baby blues affect up to 80% of new mothers and typically last 1‚Äì2 weeks. If it lasts longer or worsens, seek help. (Postpartum Support International, 2023)
  • When can I start exercising again?
    Light movement is encouraged as soon as you feel ready. For heavier exercise, wait until after your 6-week postpartum check-up. (ACOG, 2021)
  • Why am I still having cramps?
    These are called ‚Äúafterpains‚Äù as your uterus shrinks back to size. They‚Äôre more noticeable while breastfeeding. (Cleveland Clinic, 2022)
  • When will my period return?
    If you‚Äôre not breastfeeding, it may return in 6‚Äì8 weeks. If exclusively breastfeeding, it might not return for several months. (La Leche League International)
  • Can I get pregnant again right away?
    Yes. Ovulation can happen as early as 3 weeks postpartum. Discuss birth control options with your provider. (CDC, 2022)
  • Is it normal to feel pain during sex after birth?
    Yes, due to hormonal changes and healing tissues. Use lubrication and take it slow. Communicate with your partner and consult your provider if it persists. (Mayo Clinic, 2023)
  • Why does breastfeeding hurt?
    Mild discomfort at first is normal. Sharp pain, cracked nipples, or bleeding may mean baby‚Äôs latch is off. Ask a lactation consultant for help. (WHO, 2021)
  • My baby sleeps all day and is up at night‚Äîis this normal?
    Yes, many newborns have day-night confusion. Gentle light exposure during the day and quiet darkness at night can help reset their rhythm. (HealthyChildren.org)

Postpartum Recovery

  • Rest is vital‚Äîaccept help and sleep when your baby sleeps. (March of Dimes, 2021)
  • Perineal care: Rinse with warm water after using the bathroom and gently pat dry. Use witch hazel pads or sitz baths if needed. (Mayo Clinic)
  • C-section: Keep incision clean and dry. Avoid lifting anything heavier than your baby. Monitor for signs of infection. (Johns Hopkins Medicine)
  • Hydrate and eat nutritious foods to support healing. (CDC)
  • Take your prescribed medications as directed, especially pain meds or stool softeners.
  • Support your pelvic floor with Kegel exercises once cleared by your provider. (NHS UK)
  • Manage constipation with fiber-rich foods, water, and gentle movement.
  • Watch for signs of postpartum hemorrhage: sudden heavy bleeding, dizziness, or fainting. Call your provider immediately.
  • Take iron supplements if advised, especially if you experienced blood loss.
  • Attend your postpartum check-up (usually around 6 weeks). This is important for your physical and emotional health.
